# Quantify the result

The single highest-leverage fix for most behavioral answers is to end on a number. A
result the listener can measure turns a story about effort into evidence of impact.

## Find the number even when it feels unmeasurable

Candidates often say "there wasn't a metric" and stop. There almost always is one; you
just have to look one hop out:

- **Time** — "cut the deploy from 40 minutes to 6", "reviews went from 2 days to same-day".
- **Money** — revenue moved, cost avoided, churn reduced. Estimate if you must, and say
  you're estimating: "roughly $40k a week in recovered carts".
- **Volume** — requests served, tickets closed, users migrated. "Moved 1.2M rows with
  zero downtime."
- **Rate / quality** — error rate, p95 latency, test coverage, on-call pages. "Paged the
  on-call from twelve times a week to twice."
- **People** — "onboarded three engineers who each shipped in their first week."

## Say it in a way that stands up

A number only helps if it's credible. Anchor it: give the before and the after, name the
window ("within two weeks"), and don't round away honesty. "About 30% faster, measured
on our p95 dashboard over the following month" beats "way faster" and beats an
implausibly precise "31.7% faster" you can't defend.

## The fix, stated as a drill

Take any answer that ends on a vague outcome — "and that improved things", "the team was
happy" — and rewrite the last sentence as a single before→after number with its window.
If you genuinely can't find one, say what you *would* measure next time; that still shows
you think in outcomes.
